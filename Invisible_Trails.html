INTERACTIVE WEB

Invisible Trails

My thesis maps the complex digital infrastructure supporting free casual game apps on Android, aiming to demystify this complexity for users. By studying key players and their relationships, the research highlights a need for a user-friendly narrative, as users are often excluded by technical jargon meant for experts. "Invisible Trails" offers an alternative narrative to the typical app download experience, providing a space for discussion, awareness, and action. This project combines adversarial design and digital literacy to develop an educational tool that enhances users awareness, empowering them to make informed decisions.

Game apps hide a complex infrastructure driven by data-centric business models designed to collect vast amounts of user data. This opaque ecosystem conceals multiple actors, preventing users from fully understanding the system. Trackers, which are third-party tools integrated into games, address economic and functional needs by simplifying development. They operate by collecting data and remain hidden from users, seamlessly blending into the game once integrated.

To narrow down the scope, I chose to focus on the top 100 freemium casual game apps available on the Google Play Store.

GOAL

To create an alternative narrative that clearly exposes this issue while providing a space for users to independently form their own opinions.

To explore this topic, I conducted a dual-path research approach: a literature review revealed a lack of projects adequately addressing an inexperienced audience, and a digital investigation centered on several research questions con l’obiettivo di identificare gli attori del sistema, le loro relazioni e come e dove avvengono.

[A] The visualization shows the trackers headquarters, considering not only the actors but also their operating locations reflects the actual workings of a system that enables data collection, processing, and sharing worldwide

I conducted an online survey with 122 European participants to identify the audience. The survey revealed that while most users are interested in protecting their privacy, they lack the necessary knowledge to do so. Quindi ho deciso di rivolgermi a loro.

[B] Some responses to the question "How do you protect your privacy in relation to apps?"

Based on the findings, I decided to create a website to reach a broad audience, facilitating wide dissemination and engagement. The online environment directly relates to the topic, and the target audience's preference for mobile devices led to designing the site with mobile optimization in mind. Considering the target audience, I designed a visual language inspired by gaming interfaces familiar to them.

[C] The UX of the website

OUTCOME

The website "Invisible Trails" presents the findings of my research, uncovering the opaque data-centric infrastructure of game apps. The title, "Invisible Trails," plays on the dual meaning of "trail," highlighting the core aspects of the project: the data users leave behind while gaming as digital traces and the journey these data undertake. The subtitle clarifies the project's purpose and theme, making its intentions clear.

[D] The homepage offers an initial overview with all essential content structured to gradually provide necessary information for understanding the subsequent sections

The exploratory section facilitates deeper engagement with the theme. Trackers are depicted as slices, each coloured according to its category. An initial visualization organizes all identified trackers hierarchically, showcasing their variety and the most commonly used categories.
Continuing the narrative, a second visualization displays all the active trackers during the testing phase. Here, I used the patchwork metaphor to illustrate how these trackers integrate seamlessly into the game's code. Following this, the infrastructure's functioning is explained by showcasing the visual model that will be used in the specific cards for each app.
“The collection” section allows users to select a specific application and reseamble an app store to create a parallel experience able to inform the users about what they are actually downloading.
Each app's page follows the same structure, organized into sequential tabs. Here the travel metaphor serves a dual purpose: firstly, expressing the paradox that while we are “still” playing, our data travels worldwide; secondly, it helps to concretize a theme often perceived as abstract.

[E] The "About" contains all meta-project information to ensure complete accessibility and transparency throughout the process. Additionally, I chose to offer users existing tools to delve deeper into the topic

[F] The thesis book details the state-of-the-art analysis, research, concept, and design choices made for "Invisible Trails

RESULT

With "Invisible Trails," I've contributed to raising awareness about an opaque phenomenon that often excludes directly affected users from the dialogue. By employing communication design techniques and data visualization, I've created a functional and recognizable tool to address this issue.
