INTERACTIVE REPORT

Making the invisible visible

The project investigates the censorship of visual content through eight distinct visualizations, illustrating how this phenomenon operates across various platforms and affects different types of content. Our project is rooted in qualitative research, involving my team in formulating different research questions and their protocols.Each visualization addresses the theme from a unique perspective, collectively providing a comprehensive view of online censorship. This approach underscores the complexity of online censorship, highlighting the ongoing need for transparency and accountability in content regulation practices.

Before diving into the project, we had to choose a topic from those proposed. We selected the censorship of online visual content due to its opportunities for qualitative research and the use of raw data in various formats.

Online censorship involves deliberately controlling or removing accessible content driven by governments and platforms aiming to regulate public domain content. This phenomenon gains significance as user engagement deepens, sparking debate about the reasons behind content censorship, and the boundaries between appropriate and inappropriate content.

GOAL

To create a digital report featuring interactive visualizations by developing innovative research questions and protocols using digital methods.

We developed eight research questions to explore the topic from different perspectives, delving into it through three levels of analysis:

  1. The types of censored content and the different visual languages used
  2. The reactions that censorship produces, both in terms of offline movements and methods that users use to avoid it
  3. The workers behind it
I focused on the censorship of locations, starting with the research question: What is Google’s ranking of censored places on Google Maps and what kind of places are they? I began by considering the first 50 relevant results on Google search that included names or coordinates of censored places to create a dataset. I then entered the coordinates of these places into Google Maps, creating a database of images to analyze the visual languages of censorship.
Building on this data, I posed another question: How did the censorship of the researched places online change over time, and what techniques are used to censor them on Google Earth Pro? I thought it would be interesting to investigate this topic from a temporal perspective using a tool that allows the exploration of places on Earth through satellite images. I entered the coordinates of the locations into Google Earth Pro, considering the years from 2000 to 2019.

[A]A first draft of the visualization

Then, I analyzed each image to determine the method of censorship and the type of location, aiming to find correlations between the place typology and the censorship method.
I created the visualization to show how censorship techniques have changed over time. To do this, I included a navigable timeline that activates when the user clicks on one of the bars indicating the censored places.

OUTCOME

"Making the Invisible Visible" is a website showcasing eight interactive visualizations complemented by explanatory texts, key findings, and protocol schemes, offering a comprehensive view of the censorship phenomenon.

RESULT

The project emphasized the necessity for transparency and accountability in content regulation and was praised for its innovative research protocols and visualizations, providing a detailed analysis of how censorship practices and techniques have evolved over time.
